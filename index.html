The rural-to-urban continuum in the Portuguese periphery

Pedro P. Palazzo

Azores Traditional Architecture Summer School 2022

Urban process

American transect happens along the main axis.
European towns are different: there is a clear urban/rural boundary on each of the main roads. Does this mean they have no transect, as some have argued? I say the transect in colonial Portuguese towns actually happens across the major axes, in the depth of the blocks.
Before talking about a transect, we have to talk about the process of territorial development. The Atlantic islands are the first place where the Portuguese start occupying a territory that has no traces of previous human occupation.
High road, or what we call the "structuring path", near shore, but not directly on it. Other paths make inroads towards the highlands, and will reach or even cross other roads parallel to the shore (and to the contour lines). This provides the framework on which settlements will appear.
Systematic land allotment: sesmarias were established in the 14th century to redistribute fallow land in continental Portugal, so they would originally conform to and subdivide existing farmland. In the Portuguese colonies, whether on previously uninhabited lands or in regions where they conquered and enslaved the native population, the Portuguese would not acknowledge preexisting land ownership boundaries. Therefore, the sesmarias might follow somewhat more systematic dimensions. In the Azores, depending on the island and who made the allotments, a basic plot of land could range from 150 to 300 meters wide by 450 to 700 meters deep. The depth of plots would run across the terrain, often uphill from the coast but sometimes sideways from stream beds to secondary ridge lines.
This is an extreme example of a planned new town. Most settlements in the Azores are much smaller. What we see here: planned core from the beach up to the church, and two vectors of urban growth. One goes uphill to the market square, the other one is a planned extension occupying the rossio or public land reserve.
Planning not a plan: standard kit of parts and modules to be arranged according to the specific characteristics of each site.
Hamlets grow on "quiet" spots not on the hotspots (crosings).
Valley bottoms are the ultimate "backs" of the territory, but even here agricultural hamlets can form.
Then start to drift toward each other if they are around 200–600 m apart.
Finally, where they join a focal point forms with a square, perhaps a chapel, marketplace, fountain, or well.
If a spindle forms, which is to say quite often, then we can have an elongated block with very little in the way of backyards. Also note how neigboring villages begin to enclose a very large "block" of sorts that is on its way to making up an organic town.

Building types

Creep or drift of building types from the mainland to the periphery and from the urban to the rural space as villages and towns grow. The spatial cell is the basic unit, adopted from southern Portugal. It can have a structural span anywhere from 3.5 to 6.75 meters, and this is where the urban frontage modules come from. In the Azores and beyond, as we have seen, these cells will most often be arranged in a transversal row across the frontage, less often with more than one cell in depth.
This is a latecomer in the urban landscape, as it develops out of an Early Modern rural type from southern Portugal.
Small-town public buildings need not be overly articulate or even literate.
